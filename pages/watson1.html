Preface

This lesson uses As I Lay Dying and Getting Mother’s Body, to consider how one’s sense of self and identity depends upon our connections with others to make sense of our lives, especially when they feel most out of our control.

Students listen to audio clips of Faulkner’s discussion with UVA students regarding As I Lay Dying as a tour de force, and consider how his stated aim for his novel both resonates and gets transformed through Parks’s re-imagining of this family saga.

Activities

1. Faulkner in his Own Words

Study: Authors and their Words

Instructions
  1. As you read As I Lay Dying, consider it not only as a story unto itself, but also as an experiment in the implied rules (and rule-breaking!) of how one “should” write fiction. Pay attention to the kinds of formal elements Faulkner employs that draw our attention to it as a novel:
    • First-person point-of-view techniques
    • Unreliable narrators
    • Non-verbal signifiers such as drawings and numbered lists
    • Use of visual space on the page
    • Any other irregular formatting
  2. Afterwards, listen to the Digital Yoknapatawpha audio clips indicated below. Consider what his higher aim in telling a story about what it means to be a human being in a world of fellow human beings.
  3. Next, consider how Getting Mother’s Body speaks both against, but also alongside, the chief thematic and artistic aims of As I Lay Dying outlined above.Identify points of divergence and similarity, as Parks develops similar themes through different perspectives and under different circumstances.

Explore: Audio Archive

Walkthrough: Audio Archive

The Digital Yoknapatawpha Audio archive contains a curated set of audio clips taken from the larger collection available at the Faulkner at Virginia website.

  1. From the DY Main Menu, choose:
    • Indexes

  2. Click on As I Lay Dying
  3. Listen to: “Fire and flood, folly and courage” and “As tour de force”

Respond: Class Presentation and/or Learning Narrative

Questions
  1. From As I Lay Dying, choose a few scenes from Dewey Dell’s experiences of life that resonate with you as examples of someone caught in life circumstances that feel outside of their control or choosing. The scenes can be told either from her perspective or from that of other characters.
  2. Choose a few scenes from Getting Mother’s Body that “speak back” to these same aspects of Faulkner’s novel. You might, for example, consider scenes regarding unwanted pregnancy as dramatized through what Billy Beede goes through, and what we learn about Willa Mae’s life and death.
  3. From this comparison, develop an analysis that shows how each author used techniques of novel-writing to dramatize ways that human beings can try to make sense, or meaning, out of circumstances outside of their control. Note: if there is another pairing of characters that you would prefer, you are free to choose another set.

Final Product

Instructions

The final product will be a literary analysis paper that compares the narrative arcs and formal techniques of two novels, one that speak back to the other in terms of both theme and form.
