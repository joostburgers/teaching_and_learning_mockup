3. Unknown Perspectives: Tobe's Conversation

Digitized copies of an unpublished section of “A Rose for Emily” illuminate aspects of the story that are unknown to most readers. This removed section, featuring a conversation between Emily and Tobe at the end of Section Four, marks the only time in the story that Tobe speaks.

As Stephen Railton notes on the linked page to the typescript below, we do not know who decided to remove the section from the story’s publication in the April 1930 edition of Forum; however, by studying the removed section, you will reflect on the historical context of the story as well as the story’s venue of publication and its readership.

Study: “A Rose for Emily” Typescript

Instructions
  1. Study the conversation between Emily and Tobe that appears in the typescript for “A Rose for Emily”. Note that the conversation ends Section Four of the story. Available at Digital Yoknapatawpha.
  2. The Great Migration was a mass movement in which millions of African Americans relocated from the rural South to urban areas in the Northeast, Midwest, and West in the early 20th century. Driven by factors including racial violence, segregation, and better economic opportunities in industrial cities, this mass exodus fundamentally reshaped American demographics and culture.

Respond: Tobe’s Perspective

Guided Discussion
  1. How does this deleted conversation change our understanding of the story?
  2. How might Tobe's desire to move to Chicago reflect a larger theme of modernization happening in America during this period?
  3. Think about the racial prejudices of the time. How would Emily’s choice of Tobe as her heir be shocking? What does this suggest about her relationship with social norms?
  4. Why might this scene have been removed?
  5. How does removing this scene shift the story's view of modernity?

4. Visualizing Identity and Social Relations in “A Rose for Emily”

Overview

Digital Yoknapatawpha’s tools allow users to generate visual representations of social relations in each text, which you will utilize to think critically about the representations of race, class, and gender in Faulkner’s fiction.

This activity produces visualizations illustrating how the town of Jefferson interacts with Emily, even if she is isolated. It also focuses on the centering of white upper-class characters and the relative marginalization of Black characters. The activity is to encourage you to better understand the limits of Faulkner’s fiction as historical representation.

This graph shows how often characters are present or mentioned in the same event.
