Preface

This lesson is part of the Gender and Modernization cluster in the Teaching and Learning section. It examines how technological, economic, and social changes in the William Faulkner’s American South shaped gender roles.

The lesson on “Elly” can be paired with that on “A Rose for Emily”. Both stories feature women who are outcast from the town of Jefferson because they challenge traditional gender roles. In the case of “Elly”, this challenge is paradoxical. New technologies gave young women like Elly far more choices about their lives than earlier generations. At the same time, small town values and traditions could still force them to conform.

This lesson plan makes use of the following DY tools:

  • Elly (main map)
  • Character biography
  • Heatmap
  • Narrative Analysis

After this lesson, students will get a better understanding of why Elly seems to act so “irrationally”.

Tip

This module can be taught by itself or in conjunction with any stories that are part of the Faulkner and modernization cluster:

  • “Lion”
  • “A Rose for Emily”
  • “Uncle Willy”

Activities

1. Plotting the Story

Overview

In its simplest terms, “Elly” is a story about a young woman who goes against the wishes for her grandmother, and engages in a relationship with a mysterious young man, Paul de Montigny. This is not an original plot line. Romantic stories of children defying their parents or grandparents for the sake of a lover are as old as literature. What makes this story unique is how modern technology drives much of the action.

Discuss: “Elly”

As a class or in small groups do the following activities:

  1. Make a list of all the events that occur in the story.
  2. Make a list of all of the modern technologies in the story.
    • (Hint: think of anything that requires electricity, fossil fuels, or factories to make)
  3. Now draw connections between the various plot points and the different technologies that enable that action to take place.

Allow students to share their responses and note patterns in what aspects of the story they found most compelling.

3. Race and Mobility in the New South

Overview

The New South was a period of accelerated technological and economic transformation following the Civil War. The old agrarian way of life where people were rooted to the land was disrupted by industrialization in which people were increasingly mobile and less tied to specific places. Modern transportation technologies like railroads and, later, automobiles made this new economy possible.

It also meant that people in small towns increasingly came into contact with people with unknown backgrounds and ancestries. In the highly segregated South, this could lead to tensions over possible racial “passing” - when a person hides their Black ancestry by “passing” as White.

Respond: Intersecting Biographies

Guided Discussion

Ailanthia and Paul de Montigny play different roles in Elly’s life. While the grandmother can be seen as representative of the old South, Paul de Montigny represents the New South that emerges after the Civil War. Consider the following questions from this perspective.

  1. Why is it important that Ailanthia is “deaf”? What might this be symbolic of?
  2. In his biography, Paul’s racial identity is a “mystery”, but on the map he is indicated as being of mixed ancestry. Do you agree with this editorial choice? What does it mean that the town of Jefferson assumes Paul has Black ancestry even if there is no evidence?
  3. Ailanthia can trace her origins to Louisiana for four generations. Meanwhile, Paul’s origins are uncertain, he may be from Louisiana, he may have gone to Harvard and the University of Virginia. What does it say about the New South that young men like Paul are now more mobile and less rooted in a specific place, how might this disrupt the social organization of a small town like Jefferson?
  4. Elly has a lot of suitors in the story, why might she be attracted to Paul in particular? What makes him so appealing to her? Why is he more exciting than her actual fiancé, Philip?

4. Comparing “Elly” with “A Rose for Emily” - Heatmaps

Overview

The following part of the lesson compares “Elly” with “A Rose for Emily” using heatmaps to consider the different mobility women in the Old South (Emily) had versus women in the New South (Elly).

Broadly, it questions whether anything actually changes for women in the South after the Civil War.

Explore: Comparing Heatmaps

Walkthrough: Heatmaps

Each story in the main map display can be toggled to “heatmap” mode. In this mode, an overlay shows how often events occur at a particular location. When a location has a relatively high frequency count, the color shading is brighter, while lower frequency events are darker.

Heatmaps are quick way to see where most of the events take place. This does not necessarily mean that the locations with highest number of events are the most important.

Instructions
  1. From the Digital Yoknapatawpha homepage, select “Elly” from the bookshelf.
  2. On the Display Controls under Show Events, check the box next to Heatmap
  3. Compare the heatmaps between “Elly” and “A Rose for Emily”. To do this, you can open each story in a separate window and toggle the heatmap on for each story.

Respond: Heatmaps

Guided Discussion

Look at the heatmaps for “Elly” and “A Rose for Emily.” Pay special attention to the region map on the right side, and answer the questions below.

  1. What do you notice? How much more mobile is Elly than Emily?
  2. Both Elly and Emily seemed trapped, yet in entirely different ways. How does the heatmap reflect that? Where does it fall short?

5. Comparing “Elly” with “A Rose for Emily” - Narrative Analysis

Overview

The following part of the lesson compares “Elly” with “A Rose for Emily” using narrative analysis to consider the different narrative structures of each story. It considers how Emily is “stuck” in the past, while Elly is trying to go to a future that does not exist yet.

Explore: Narrative Analysis

Walkthrough: Narrative Analysis Visualizations

The narrative analysis visualization tool uses the event data from the main map to create a graphical representation of the plot structure.

Chronological order is indicated on the y-axis and events that happen later are higher on the axis.

Page order is represented on the x-axis and moves from the first page to the last page from left to right

Instructions
  1. From the DY Main Menu choose:
    • Visualizations
    • Narrative Analysis
  2. Select “A Rose for Emily” from the drop-down menu on the left.
  3. Hover over each chart to see more detailed information about each data point
  4. Click “Add Chart”
  5. Select “Elly” from the new drop-down menu
  6. Click “additional insights” on “Elly” to see narrative status, narrative progression, and event frequency by date.

Respond: Narrative Analysis

Guided Discussion

The narrative analysis charts can be quite overwhelming. The key feature to look for is whether a chart has a regular stair-step pattern or is “scrambled”. If there is a regular slope from the bottom left to the top right, the story is generally told in chronological order. If, on the other hand, the chart jumps up and down on the Y-axis as it moves forward on the x-axis, this means that there are a lot of flashbacks and flashfowards. This indicates that the text is not told in chronological order.

  1. Which of these stories is mostly told in chronological order? Which of these stories is told out of order?
  2. Why might one story be told out of order and the other in linear fashion? How might this relate to their main characters, Emily and Elly?
  3. Clicking on “Date Order” for both stories reveals when in time they take place. Which of these stories is set in the past and which is set in the present?
  4. The stories are set in different times. Emily’s affair with Homer Barron is in the 1880s and Elly’s dalliance with Paul is in the 1920s. In both stories, the women cannot find lovers their families (Elly) or communities (Emily) will accept. Is there a difference between what type of men are “out of bounds” for each woman? What does that difference tell us about shifting social relations?
  5. Does this shift show progress or is it simply a different type of constraint?

Final Product

Cluster Analysis

The cluster of lessons about gender and modernization includes:

  • “Elly”
  • “Lion”
  • “A Rose for Emily”
  • “Uncle Willy”

While each story had its own unique themes, in all of them characters were confronted with changing social norms due to modernization. Elly tried to be the adventurous “New Woman”. Emily tried to stick to tradition after the Civil War and ended up , until she found Homer. The church women of Jefferson tried to make Uncle Willy more respectable. Meanwhile, the death of Old Ben and Lion symbolize the death of untouched nature, a space outside of the civilized world where the men of Jefferson could go to reconnect with each other and themselves.

For all their similarities about changing social roles, these stories are also different in their outcomes. Although none end happily, some end worse than others. Likewise, the characters inhabit different spaces and represent different generations. Plotting these positions on a cluster chart can help us discover the politics of a text. Here politics does not mean a specific political party, but rather the form of social organization the stories see as positive and negative.

A bi-axial (two axes) cluster chart tries to visualize these positions. Arguably, you could use any two categories to make a chart like this. A common one is one that frames politics as a choice between more or less economic regulation and more or less social regulation by the government.

To help map out these stories, the two oppositional axes are “Nature vs. Modernization” and “Traditional vs. non-Traditional Gender Roles”. In the case of the former, characters are plotted from left to right based on whether they seem to prefer nature or the way of life modernization provides. The Y-axis opposes traditional vs. non-traditional gender roles. Traditional gender roles in these stories are characters who marry and have children or think it is important for others to do so. Non-traditional gender roles is any character who does not do what society expects of men and women.

Instructions
  • Drag each character from the Character Bank into the quadrant that best represents their relationship to nature vs. modernization and traditional vs. non-traditional gender roles.
  • Double-click a placed character to return it to the bank.
  • Click Save as Image to download your completed chart.
  • Compare your chart with a classmate, group, or post it on a discussion board.

Guided Questions
  • Why did you organize the chart in the way you did?
  • Is there any particular character who stands out as not being like the others?
  • Does this character fare better than the other characters?
  • What kinds of characters tend to have the worst end?
  • If we assume that characters who end badly represent a certain limit to what is possible in a society, what are the politics of Yoknapatawpha? Does it promote modernization and non-traditional gender roles or is it better to maintain traditional gender roles and be skeptical of modernization?
  • Generally, do men or women fare better or do both suffer bad fates equally?
  • Is it more difficult for male or female characters to adapt to shifting gender roles in the face of a rapidly modernizing world?
