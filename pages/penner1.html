Collaborating on a Genealogy for  As I Lay Dying

Description:
Since each member of the Bundren family in As I Lay Dying has a different view of what it means to be a “family,” in this project we replace a one-size-fits-all genealogical chart with an evolving genealogy that reflects characters’ changing perspectives as the story unfolds.  This is a multi-media project that connects writing and visual art, and pairs at-home student work with in-class collaboration and presentation.
Modality:

In-Person

Lesson Plan

Preface

Each student will create a one-slide visual representation of the Bundren family as it appears to one character at one point in time in the narrative. The student will write a short “tombstone” or wall description (as you might see in a museum) to accompany the slide, explaining their aesthetic and analytic choices, and including textual support. After the instructor collates the slides into a single PowerPoint, students will take turns presenting their image to the class, after which the class can compare approaches and the many Bundren conceptions of "family."

Activities

Activity: Faulkner’s Family Genealogies

Explore: Faulkner’s Family Genealogies
In-class Preparation
Walkthrough

  1. Visit the “Yoknapatawpha's First Families” section of the DY site. Look at, for example, how the Compson family genealogy changes depending on which texts you select: The Sound and the Fury, with its focus on a nuclear family, as opposed to the “Appendix,” which sets that nuclear family in a much longer genealogical line. There is no DY representation of the Bundren family genealogy, since it appears in only one Faulkner text. But what we know of the Bundren genealogy changes over the course of the novel, as we shift character perspectives and learn more family secrets. Considering how the DY editors have tracked Faulkner’s families across texts and time, the class is ready to make their own evolving genealogy for As I Lay Dying.

Questions for in-class discussion:
  • What tools are appropriate for this novel?
  • How does his story make us newly aware of genealogies as constructed documents?
  • Does he uphold the methods and values inherent in such scholarly charts?
Respond: Create Your Own Bundren Genealogy Contribution
Out-of-Class Exercise
Instructions

Each student will create a one-slide visual representation of the Bundren family as it appears to one character at one point in time in the narrative. The student will also write a short “tombstone” or wall description (as you might see in a museum) to accompany the slide, explaining their aesthetic choices, and including textual support and analysis.

Questions to consider
  • What does The Bundren Family genealogy depicted here tell us about the point in time or point of view from which the family tree is constructed? What are the values reflected in this genealogy? Are they shared by Faulkner’s characters?
  • How do we depict marital and extra-marital relationships?
  • Does the usual emphasis on progeny and patriarchy capture a given character’s way of building meaning in his or her life?
  • Is birth order the right way of signaling a child’s relationship with parents and siblings?
  • Can an individual flee the family tree?
  • Are all characters clear on the distinction between people and property? Are there ways of indicating relationship that don’t slide into proprietary claims? (“This is my child” is grammatically indistinguishable from “This is my chocolate.”)
  • Where does Addie go when she dies? Does she remain visible in your representation of the family?
  • Are you linking people to people? people to animals? people to land? Are you highlighting social, moral, aesthetic, or political relationships, or something else entirely?

Final Product

The instructor will compile the students’ individual slides into a single PowerPoint presentation, following the event order of the novel, and hang the tombstones around the classroom. Each student will then have an opportunity to present their slide and tombstone to the class, after which students will discuss how different characters or different points in time foreground different priorities in structuring the Bundren characters as “family.”
