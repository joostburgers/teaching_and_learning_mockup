Preface

This lesson plan examines William Faulkner's portrayal of the American South in transition, focusing on how modernity functions as both liberator and prison for characters like Elly, whose experiences reveal the contradictory nature of change. It will analyze how technological innovations can transform social dynamics and personal agency while simultaneously creating new limitations, as well as explore Faulkner's complex presentation of race that exposes the hierarchies omnipresent in Southern society, and investigate how women navigate limited options within a patriarchal structure, despite modernity's promises of progress. By examining these themes will learn to recognize that liberation in one area often coincides with new forms of restriction in others; a paradox that resonates with our contemporary life./p>

3. Limitations of Progress

Respond: Ambiguity of Race

Guided Discussion

Paul’s race is left ambiguous in the text. Elly’s grandmother, insists he has “negro blood” but Elly insists to her grandmother that Paul attended Harvard and the University of Virginia, the latter of which wouldn’t admit African Americans for another 3 decades.

  1. What other evidence suggests Paul's race? Why might Faulkner keep this ambiguous?
  2. How would reading Paul as a different race affect our interpretation of the story?
  3. What does Elly's grandmother's reaction to Paul reveal about Southern racial attitudes?
  4. How might class intersect with race in this story?

Respond: Descriptions of Elly’s romantic life

Guided Discussion

Elly’s given name, Ailanthia, is taken from her grandmother, yet the two women could not be further apart. Elly's romantic encounters on the veranda and her intense pursuit of Paul de Montigny can be interpreted through multiple lenses— as expressions of autonomy, as weapons of rebellion, or as manifestations of deeper psychological needs.

  1. Does Elly’s love seem genuine? Or is it performative?
  2. In what ways does the grandmother's surveillance shape Elly's romantic endeavors?
  3. How does Faulkner connect Elly's behavior to broader themes of power, control, and self-destruction in the story?
  4. How might Elly represent the New South? How might her grandmother represent the old?

4. Comparing "Elly" with "A Rose for Emily"

Overview

Comparing "A Rose for Emily" and "Elly" offers a revealing lens into modernism's dual fascination with time, as Emily represents the Southern past's persistent grip while Elly embodies an anxiety about a rapidly approaching future. These two modules demonstrate how Faulkner used different techniques to explore the same fundamental modernist concern: individuals trapped between collapsing traditions and uncertain futures.

Explore: Comparing Heatmaps

Discussion: Heatmaps

Look at the heat maps for “Elly” and “A Rose for Emily.” Pay special attention to the region map on the right side, and answer the questions below.

  1. What do you notice? How much more mobile is Elly than Emily?
  2. Both Elly and Emily seemed trapped, yet in entirely different ways. How does the heat map reflect that? Where does it fall short?
