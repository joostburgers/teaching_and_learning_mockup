Preface

This lesson plan examines William Faulkner's portrayal of the American South in transition, focusing on how modernity functions as both liberator and prison for characters like Elly, whose experiences reveal the contradictory nature of social change. It will analyze how technological innovations transform social dynamics and personal agency while simultaneously creating new limitations, explore Faulkner's complex presentation of race that exposes the hierarchies omnipresent in Southern society, and investigate how women navigate limited options within a patriarchal structure, despite modernity's promises of progress.

Teaching Tips for how to teach this lesson:

  • Use visualization tools to help students identify patterns in character relationships
  • Encourage students to consider both textual evidence and the historical context of the American South

Activities

1. Opening activity

Study: "Elly"

Opening Questions
  • What happens in the story?
  • Does Elly live?
  • How do Elly's attitudes toward freedom affect her throughout the story?

Allow students to share their responses and note patterns in what aspects of the story they found most compelling.

Respond: Heatmap

Respond and examine the various locations in the story. Pay special attention to the “regional” map in the top right-hand corner.

Questions
  • Where does the story take place? Where outside of Jefferson?
  • How do the characters get to Mills City? How do they get back?
  • Is the automobile a symbol of freedom or danger for Elly? Or both?
  • The story opens with an automobile driving dangerously close to a high ledge. How might this be relevant today? What modern technologies both inhibit and enable freedom?

3. Limitations of Progress

Examine: Ambiguity of Race

Guided Discussion

Paul’s race is left ambiguous in the text. Elly’s grandmother, insists he has “negro blood” but Elly insists to her grandmother that Paul attended Harvard and the University of Virginia, the latter of which wouldn’t admit African Americans for another 3 decades.

  1. What other evidence suggests Paul's race? Why might Faulkner keep this ambiguous?
  2. How would reading Paul as a different race affect our interpretation of the story?
  3. What does Elly's grandmother's reaction to Paul reveal about Southern racial attitudes?
  4. How might class intersect with race in this story?

Examine: descriptions of Elly’s romantic life

Guided Discussion

Elly’s given name, Ailanthia, is taken from her grandmother, yet the two women could not be further apart. Elly's romantic encounters on the veranda and her intense pursuit of Paul de Montigny can be interpreted through multiple lenses— as expressions of autonomy, as weapons of rebellion, or as manifestations of deeper psychological needs.

  1. Does Elly’s love seem genuine? Or is it performative?
  2. In what ways does the grandmother's surveillance shape Elly's romantic endeavors?
  3. What does Elly's grandmother's reaction to Paul reveal about Southern racial attitudes?
  4. How does Faulkner connect Elly's sexual behavior to broader themes of power, control, and self-destruction in the story?

4. Comparing "Elly" with "A Rose for Emily"

Overview

Digital Yoknapatawpha’s tools allow users to generate visual representations of social relations in each text, which you will utilize to think critically about the representations of race, class, and gender in Faulkner’s fiction.

This activity produces visualizations illustrating the story’s centering of white upper-class characters and relative marginalization of working class and Black characters. The activity is to encourage you to better understand the limits of Faulkner’s fiction as historical representation.

Explore: Character Connections

Instructions
  1. Click "Visualization" on the navigation bar at the top of the Digital Yoknapatawpha homepage
    • Click Character-Character
  2. Set Text as “A Rose for Emily”
  3. Complete the following searches and answer the related questions:
    1. All characters
      • Set Text as “A Rose for Emily”
      • Leave all other fields blank
      • Click Search
    2. Race
      • Set Race as “White”
      • Click Search
      • Repeat for each race
      • Switch Rank between “Major” and “Major and Secondary”
        Questions
        • Approximately how many total characters appear as white?
        • How many total characters appear as Black?
        • Which major and secondary characters appear as white?
        • Which major and secondary characters appear as Black?
    3. Gender
      • Set Gender as “Male”
      • Click Search
      • Repeat for each gender
        Questions
        • About how many male characters appear in the story, and how are they connected?
        • About how many female characters appear in the story, and how are they connected?
    4. Class
      • Set Class as “Upper Class”
      • Click Search
      • Repeat for each class
        Questions
        • Which characters appear according to each social class designation: upper class, middle class, lower class, free Black?
    5. Other
      • Select any remaining variables
      • Click Search
        Question
        • What data appears in your search?

Respond: Social Relations

Instructions

Respond and reflect on the story by describing what appears in the search results in terms of race, gender, and class relations.

Questions
Is there a way to link these questions to traditional and modern womanhood? Keep in mind that the story is not told from Emily's perspective, but from that of the town who is focused on emily.
  • Compare the number of characters by race, gender, and class categories
  • What is the likely demographic of the story's narrator based on connections?
  • How would the story differ if told from Tobe's or Homer's perspective?
  • What are the strengths and limitations of Faulkner's character choices?
