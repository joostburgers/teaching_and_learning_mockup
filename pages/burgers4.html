DIGITAL Yoknapatawpha · Teaching & Learning

Preface

William Faulkner rewrote the story of "Lion" multiple times, and the material would ultimately become reworked in a much longer story called "The Bear" in Go Down, Moses!. Although shorter and less canonical, "Lion" covers many similar themes to "The Bear", and in particular provides an extensive meditation on one boy's coming of age in the primeval forest. In doing so, it explores traditional masculinity in the face of an encroaching modernity.

The module will use the following tools to explore these themes:

  • Main Map - Characters
  • Characters by Location in Events Graph
  • Cumulative Locations

By the end of the lesson, students will have considered how masculine identity in Faulkner is framed in opposition to modernization and as a retreat back into the wilderness.

Tip

This module can be taught by itself or in conjunction with any stories that are part of the Faulkner and modernization cluster:

Activities

1. Main Characters in “Lion”

Overview

Faulkner rewrote the story of "Lion" several times. Each instance features a young narrator surrounded by a motley group of characters who normally would not cross paths on the streets of Jefferson. In the woods they become a temporary fraternity of hunters. Knowing their unique backgrounds helps to better their shared masculinity.

Explore: Character Biography

Walkthrough: Main Map - Characters

The main map display shows all the locations and characters in the text. The events can be played using the toolbar at the bottom. Each location and character can be filtered down through the options on toolbar on the left side and the locations and characters can all be clicked to provide more information.

Instructions
  1. From the Digital Yoknapatawpha homepage, select “Lion” from the bookshelf.
  2. Select "Major & Secondary" radio button on the Display Controls
  3. Find the following characters:
    • “Ad” ()
    • “Boon Hogganbeck”()
    • “Major De Spain”()
    • “Quentin”()
  4. Click on the icon
    • A biography record will pop up in a new window
  5. Read the description of each character

Respond: The Wilderness Men

Guided Discussion

The character overviews should give a better sense of the diversity of the cast. As the owner of the land, De Spain certainly has the most authority, but it is interesting to consider what roles the other characters play.

  • Based on what we are told about Boon in his description and his actions in the story, why might it be odd that he develops a bond with Ad?
  • Why is it that Quentin, still a teenager, is allowed to associate with these rough and tumble men?
  • What do you note about the racial diversity of the characters?
  • Lion and Old Ben are not listed as characters because Digital Yoknapatawpha does not keep track of animals. What arguments can you make for including them?

2. Characters at Events

Overview

Faulkner's portrayal of early twentieth century Southern society is very hierarchical. This is most present in his depictions of race, but there are also more subtle ways in which class and gender norms follow rigid social codes. Yet, the enforcement of any social code is inherently contextual. How students interact in the classroom is different than how they might interact at a party. Similarly, Faulkner's characters act differently to one another depending on the context of the story. Faulkner's hunting stories are no different and represent a social world quite different than the one that is predominant in Jefferson.

Explore: Character Context

Walkthrough: Characters by Location in Events Graph

Individual character profiles can give a good overview of the kinds of characters in a particular story, but these do not capture larger patterns. The Character in Location by Event graph allows users to group characters by similar features and gives a better overview of what kinds of characters tend to appear where.

The graph allows users to show the distribution of characters at locations by type through different drop filters. By cycling through different filters, users can establish what types of characters appear at what locations and how frequently. The frequency gives some idea of the dominance of a particular type of character at a specific location.

Instructions
  1. Click "Visualization" on the navigation bar at the top of the Digital Yoknapatawpha homepage
    • Click Character-Event
  2. Set Text as “Lion”
  3. Complete the following searches and make a note of the type and frequency of locations for each demographic group:
    1. Gender
      • Set Gender as “Male”
      • Click Search
      • Repeat for each gender
    2. Race
      • Set Race as “White”
      • Click Search
      • Repeat for “Black” and “Mixed Indian White”
Guided Questions
  1. What gender is predominant in the story?
  2. How frequently do women appear in this story, and where do they appear?
  3. The data suggest a strong gender divide in locations. Men are present in both nature (the hunting camp and surrounding areas) and culture (man-made sites where people live), while women are only present in places of culture. Why do you think this is?
  4. How might the division between nature and culture break along racial lines? Do different occur together more in nature or culture?
  5. If we assume that "nature" is better for racial relationships, what does this say about the encroachments of culture on the woods where the story takes place?
  6. What appears to be the source of racial conflict?

3. Deep Histories of Place

Overview

Faulkner's Yoknapatawpha stories are set in the same county over a period of many years and stretch from the end of the 18th century all the way to the middle of the 20th century. Consequently, events in one story can take place in the same locations as another story. As they do so, locations take on new and different meanings over time.

The cumulative locations in Digital Yoknapatawpha captures all the ways in which different locations occur throughout the stories. This is especially useful if you are just starting your journey in Faulkner's world. Knowing that a particular location was the site of an important event in another story, helps situate one text within the larger saga.

Explore: Cumulative Locations

Walkthrough: Cumulative Locations
Cumulative locations can be accessed either through the Search drop-down in the main menu bar or by clicking on the cumulative Location link within a specific record. These instructions use the second approach.
  1. Navigate to the Digital Yoknapatawpha home page
  2. Select “Lion” from the bookshelf
    • This will open the main map display for “Lion”
  3. Select the “Event Locations radio button
  4. Click on “Hunting Camp” ()
  5. Scroll to the bottom of the record that pops up
  6. Click on cumulative Location data
  7. Read the description
  8. Repeat the steps for the following locations:
    • “Holly Knoll” ()
    • “The Woods”()
    • “Logging Railroad”()
    • “Hoke's”()

Respond: The Masculinity and Progress

Guided Discussion

Reviewing the cumulative Location entries should reveal that the forest "primeval" is not as untouched as Quentin imagines in "Lion". Later it will be revealed that this is actually the site of a former plantation and that a logging company is slowly encroaching on the "Big Woods." By the end of the Yoknapatawpha saga, hunters will have to travel farther to get to the woods because they are being destroyed. Yet, in "Lion" the fantasy of virgin land is still very much alive.

  • From the story, it is clear that many of the men have a life outside the woods. De Spain is a wealthy businessman, Boon is the town reprobate, and Quentin has a family. Why is it important that the men participate in a hunt every year? And why is it important that Quentin participates in a hunt to become a man?
  • Did De Spain mean for Old Ben and Lion to die during the hunt or was it a ritual? How might the death of Old Ben and Lion have changed the meaning of the woods for Major De Spain?
  • How does the presence of the logging company in the background of the story tie in with the loss of masculine spaces?
  • Are there still spaces where teenage boys can participate in a masculinity ritual or have these all been through modernization?
  • Quentin's version of the Big Woods is an idyllic space, where men find a common bond and ignore the barriers of race and class that exist in the civilized world of Jefferson. Do you agree with this? Has modernization created barriers between people? Or is this simply a fantasy that ignores how race and gender discrimination have allowed men like De Spain to become wealthy enough to buy a hunting preserve where he can pretend race, class, and gender difference does not exist?

4. Comparing "Uncle Willy" with "Lion" - Heatmaps

Overview

The following part of the lesson compares "Uncle Willy" with "Lion" using heatmaps to consider how masculinity is framed in different contexts.

Broadly, it interrogates how masculinity in the primitive wilderness is different than masculinity in a modern town.

Explore: Comparing Heatmaps

Walkthrough: Heatmaps

Each story in the main map display can be toggled to "heatmap" mode. In this mode, an overlay shows how often events occur at a particular location. When a location has a relatively high frequency count, the color shading is brighter, while lower frequency events are darker.

Heatmaps are a quick way to see where most of the events take place. This does not necessarily mean that the locations with highest number of events are the most important.

Instructions
  1. From the Digital Yoknapatawpha homepage, select “Uncle Willy” from the bookshelf.
  2. On the Display Controls under Show Events, check the box next to Heatmap
  3. Compare the heatmaps between "Uncle Willy" and "Lion". To do this, you can open each story in a separate window and toggle the heatmap on for each story.

Respond: Heatmaps

Guided Discussion

Look at the heat maps for “Uncle Willy” and “Lion.” For "Uncle Willy", make a note of the fact that a substantial part of the action happens in or around Memphis. This is visible through the Memphis inset and the events in the region map. Meanwhile, note that in "Lion" there is no inset on the map, meaning that all the action is taking place in the "Big Bottom", hunting grounds to the northwest of town.

  1. In "Uncle Willy", Memphis becomes a place for him to live out his increasingly debauched lifestyle of drinking and spending time with sex workers. Meanwhile, in "Lion" the men turn to the wilderness to drink and hunt. How do these different social spaces suggest what is and what is not appropriate for modern men?
  2. Both stories seem to "escape" small town Jefferson. Why do you think small town respectability could also be constraining for men?

5. Comparing “Uncle Willy” and “Lion” - Character Demographics

Overview

Although a very much outmoded way of thinking now, Faulkner's Yoknapatawpha County can be broken down into five distinct racial groups:

  • White () - The largest social group
  • Black () - The second largest social group
  • Native Americans () - A much smaller group
  • Mixed Race ()- An even smaller group, consisting of characters like Boon Hogganbeck who have ancestry from different racial groups
  • Asian () - Very rare mentions only

Digital Yoknapatawpha made the conscious decision to represent Faulkner's racial groups through very distinct icons to capture his vision, even if it might make modern readers uncomfortable.

As his fiction takes place between the early 19th century and the middle of the twentieth century many of his stories are either explicitly or implicitly about the relationship between White and Black characters during enslavement and, later, segregation in the Jim Crow South. Faulkner views these relationships largely from a White male perspective. Indeed, the overwhelming majority of events in the fiction feature White men. Nevertheless, he is interested in creating situations in his fictions in which characters cross the "color line", and transgress the rigid racial hierarchy of the segregated South. These tensions are often visible in the demographics of his stories.

Walkthrough: Main Map - Character Demographics

The main map display shows all the locations and characters in the text. The events can be played using the toolbar at the bottom. Respond: Race and Space

Guided Discussion

One of the most immediately noticeable differences between the two stories is that "Lion" is far more diverse in terms of the different races present. What is more, White people, while still a majority in "Lion" are far less dominant than in "Uncle Willy", which is almost entirely White.

Less quickly visible is the gender ratio. There are virtually no women present in "Lion." Meanwhile, there are far more women in "Uncle Willy". They are, after all, the ones who give him so much grief.

  1. Why is "Lion" so much more racially diverse than "Uncle Willy"? How might this relate to the setting and the action in the story?
  2. What does it say about Uncle Willy that in a majority White story he is close to the Black characters?
  3. Why are there so many more women in "Uncle Willy" than in "Lion"?
  4. Where do men seem happiest in these stories? What does that say about Faulkner's notion of masculinity?
  5. For Faulkner, is the "modernization" of men into prudent, middle-class, professional members of a town positive? Or does he see modernization as detrimental to masculinity?

Final Product

Cluster Analysis

The cluster of lessons about gender and modernization includes:

  • “Elly”
  • “Lion”
  • “A Rose for Emily”
  • “Uncle Willy”

While each story had its own unique themes, in all of them characters were confronted with changing social norms due to modernization. Elly tried to be the adventurous "New Woman". Emily tried to stick to tradition after the Civil War and ended up alone. The church women of Jefferson tried to make Uncle Willy more respectable. Meanwhile, the death of Old Ben and Lion symbolize the death of untouched nature, a space outside of the civilized world where the men of Jefferson could go to reconnect with each other and themselves.

For all their similarities about changing social roles, these stories are also different in their outcomes. Although none end happily, some end more badly than others. Likewise, the characters inhabit different spaces and represent different generations. Plotting these positions on a cluster chart can help us discover the politics of a text. Here politics does not mean a specific political party, but rather the form of social organization the stories see as positive and negative.

A bi-axial (two axes) cluster chart tries to visualize conceptualize these positions. Arguably, you could use any two categories to make a chart like this. A common one is one that frames politics as a choice between more or less economic regulation and more or less social regulation by the government.

To help map out these stories, the two oppositional axes are “Nature vs. Modernization” and “Traditional vs. non-Traditional Gender Roles”. In the case of the former, characters are plotted from left to right based on whether they seem to prefer nature or the way of life modernization provides. The Y-axis opposes traditional vs. non-traditional gender roles. Traditional gender roles in these stories are characters who marry and have children or think it is important for others to do so. Non-traditional gender roles is any character who does not do what society expects of men and women.

Instructions
  • Drag each character from the Character Bank into the quadrant that best represents their relationship to nature vs. modernization and traditional vs. non-traditional gender roles.
  • Double-click a placed character to return it to the bank.
  • Click Save as Image to download your completed chart.
  • Compare your chart with a classmate, group, or post it on a discussion board.

Guided Questions
  • Why did you organize the chart in the way you did?
  • Is there any particular character who stands out as not being like the others?
  • Does this character fare better than the other characters?
  • What kinds of characters tend to have the worst end?
  • If we assume that characters who end badly represent a certain limit to what is possible in a society, what are the politics of Yoknapatawpha? Does it promote modernization and non-traditional gender roles or is it better to maintain traditional gender roles and be skeptical of modernization?
  • Generally, do men or women fare better or do both suffer bad fates equally?
  • Is it more difficult for male or female characters to adapt to shifting gender roles in the face of a rapidly modernizing world?
