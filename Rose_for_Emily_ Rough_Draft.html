A Rose for Emily: Intention vs. Meaning

Overview

This lesson explores William Faulkner's "A Rose for Emily" through authorial intention versus reader interpretation, with special attention given to narrative gaps and the portrayal of gender, race, and class through digital humanities tools.

Learning Goals

  • Distinguish between authorial intention and reader interpretation
  • Analyze narrative gaps using digital visualization tools
  • Examine social dynamics through character relationships and narrative structure
  • Explore archival materials that reveal alternative versions of the text

Lesson Plan

Opening Activity

  • Opening Salvo: "What makes a story haunting? Which moment in 'A Rose for Emily' lingered with you, and why?"

Narrative Structure Analysis

  1. Digital Timeline Exploration:
    • Students access Digital Yoknapatawpha's Narrative Analysis tool for "A Rose for Emily"
    • Examine the chronological vs. page order timeline
    • Discussion questions:
      • Is the story told chronologically?
      • Why does Faulkner use flashbacks rather than linear storytelling?
      • How does this non-linear structure affect our understanding of Emily?
  2. Quick Share:
    • Students share observations about how the narrative structure shapes reader interpretation

Unkown Perspectives: Tobe's Conversation

  1. Archival Discovery:
    • Distribute the typescript excerpt containing the unpublished conversation between Emily and Tobe
    • Students read in small groups, noting:
      • What we learn about Tobe's perspective and plans to move to Chicago
      • Why Tobe refuses to inherit Emily's house
      • How this conversation connects to the Great Migration
  2. Guided Discussion:
    • How does this deleted conversation change our understanding of the story?
    • What aspects of American history become visible with this conversation?
    • Do you think the published version is more effective with or without this scene?
    • What might have motivated its removal before publication?

Character Connections and Social Relations

  1. Digital Visualization Activity:
    • Students access DY's Character-Character Force Directed Graph tool
    • In small groups, students conduct searches by:
      • Race (White vs. Black characters)
      • Gender (Male vs. Female characters)
      • Class (Upper, Middle, Lower, Free Black)
  2. Analysis Questions:
    • Compare the number of characters by race, gender, and class categories
    • What is the likely demographic of the story's narrator based on connections?
    • How would the story differ if told from Tobe's or Homer's perspective?
    • What are the strengths and limitations of Faulkner's character choices?

Closing Activity

  1. Thesis Development:
    • Students draft a preliminary thesis statement connecting any of the explored aspects:
      • Character identities and social relations
      • Historical context revealed through archival materials
      • Narrative structure and its effects on interpretation
      • Authorial intention vs. reader interpretation
  2. Homework Assignment:
    • Listen to the Digital Yoknapatawpha audio recording of Faulkner
    • Read "Elly" by William Faulkner
